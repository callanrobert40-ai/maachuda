Country Sourdough Bread

Active: 30 min • Ferment: 12-18 hrs • Bake: 45 min • Yield: 1 loaf

There's nothing quite like pulling a golden, crackling sourdough loaf from your own oven. This recipe is designed for beginners — no fancy equipment needed beyond a Dutch oven and a little patience. The long, cold fermentation develops complex, tangy flavors while creating the open, airy crumb and crispy crust that define great sourdough. Once you bake your first loaf, you'll never go back to store-bought bread.

Ingredients

  • 375g bread flour (or strong white flour)
  • 125g whole wheat flour
  • 100g active sourdough starter (fed 4-8 hours before, bubbly and risen)
  • 350ml lukewarm water (about 30°C / 86°F)
  • 10g fine sea salt
  • Rice flour for dusting (prevents sticking to the banneton)

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Autolyse (30 min): In a large bowl, combine the bread flour and whole wheat flour. Add lukewarm water and mix with your hands until no dry flour remains. It will look shaggy — that's perfect. Cover with a damp towel and let rest for 30 minutes. This step hydrates the flour and begins gluten development automatically.
  2. Add starter and salt: After autolyse, add the active sourdough starter and salt on top of the dough. Use the pinch-and-fold method: pinch the dough between your thumb and fingers, then fold it over. Rotate the bowl and repeat for 3-4 minutes until the starter and salt are fully incorporated.
  3. Bulk fermentation with stretch-and-folds (4-5 hours): Cover the bowl. Over the next 2 hours, perform 4 sets of stretch-and-folds, one every 30 minutes. For each set: wet your hand, slide it under one side of the dough, stretch it up, and fold it over the center. Rotate 90° and repeat 4 times. After the last fold, let the dough rest undisturbed for the remaining 2-3 hours. The dough is ready when it has risen by about 50%, feels airy and jiggly, and shows bubbles on the surface.
  4. Pre-shape: Gently turn the dough onto a lightly floured surface. Using a bench scraper and your hands, shape it into a loose round. Don't deflate it — use gentle movements. Let it rest on the counter for 20 minutes (bench rest).
  5. Final shape: Lightly flour the top of the dough. Flip it over (floured side down). Pull the bottom edge up to the center, then the left side, then the right side, then the top — like folding an envelope. Flip the dough seam-side down and use your hands to gently drag it toward you on the counter, building surface tension. Place seam-side up in a banneton basket generously dusted with rice flour.
  6. Cold proof (12-18 hours): Cover the banneton with a damp towel or plastic wrap. Place in the refrigerator for 12-18 hours. The cold proof slows fermentation and develops the tangy sourdough flavor and open crumb.
  7. Preheat and bake: Place your Dutch oven (with lid) in a cold oven. Preheat to 250°C (480°F) for 45 minutes — the pot must be screaming hot. Carefully remove the dough from the fridge. Turn it out onto a sheet of parchment paper. Score the top with a razor blade or sharp knife in one decisive slash at a 45° angle, about 1 cm deep.
  8. Bake covered: Carefully lower the dough (on parchment) into the hot Dutch oven. Cover with the lid and bake for 20 minutes. The lid traps steam, creating the crispy, blistered crust.
  9. Bake uncovered: Remove the lid. Reduce heat to 230°C (445°F). Bake for another 20-25 minutes until the crust is deep golden brown and the loaf sounds hollow when tapped on the bottom.
  10. Cool: Transfer to a wire rack and let cool for at least 1 hour before slicing. This is the hardest part — but the crumb is still setting inside. Cutting too early will result in a gummy texture.

Tips & Variations

  • Don't have a starter? Make one from scratch in 7 days: combine 50g flour + 50g water in a jar. Every 24 hours, discard half, then feed with 50g flour + 50g water. By day 5-7, it should be reliably doubling in size within 4-8 hours.
  • The float test: To check if your starter is active enough, drop a small spoonful into a glass of water. If it floats, it's ready to bake with.
  • No Dutch oven? Use a large oven-safe pot with a lid, or bake on a pizza stone with a tray of boiling water in the bottom of the oven for steam.
  • Flour matters: Bread flour (high protein, 12-14%) produces the best gluten structure. All-purpose flour works but gives a denser crumb.
